Academic Practice

I understand that I must conduct good academic practice when working on and submitting my assignments. The Falmouth University academic integrity policy states “All use of another person’s work or ideas must be attributed and the sources identified, including where you have paraphrased or summarised. You must make sure that you use the correct techniques for citation and referencing as outlined in the assessment brief.” (Falmouth University, 2020). In my writing and practical work which consists of image and asset creation etc I will ensure to cite the correct author or creator to ensure good academic practice and to give credit where it is due. 

I will ensure that credit is given in relation to my games development practices where required as there has been many cases over the years of lawsuits caused by plagiarism of game content, one in particular being the 1972 lawsuit of Magnavox Vs Atari where Ralph Baer, inventor of the Magnavox Odyssey games console sought legal action against Atari for claiming that the Atari co-founder Nolan Bushnell had copied Ping-Pong and in conclusion Atari continued to sell the game on arcades and home consoles while paying the licensing and royalty fees to Magnavox (Obias, 2014). This event is important to reflect on as a video games programmer and developer it is paramount that I do not attempt to copy or steal any content that I use in my game projects, and I will ensure that I will purchase the appropriate license for assets or them from the ground up to avoid any lawsuits for future practice.

Creativity

The term creativity means to stop outside of our current context and reimagine the world around us. With creativity we can apply ideas through creative expression. “Through the ‘creative process’ we are modelling, testing, iterating, re-modelling and building up ideas – essentially bringing imagination into a reality.” (Doust, 2019). As a game’s developer and programmer, I utilize the creative process to write game mechanics that generate game events which in turn provoke emotions in players. Emotions are one of the great unacknowledged secrets of game design and what matters the most in video games is how they make us feel (Sylvester, 2013).

Rapid Ideation

The definition of Rapid ideation is “To generate ideas in a short amount of time, explore alternative solutions, consider insights from a broader group, and build enthusiasm.” (Innovation Champions, 2021), for video games the main two rapid ideation techniques are game jams and hackathons. A games jam is for teams to come together and make a video game, non-digital game or card game where participants design and inject new ideas to grow the global games industry (Global Game Jam, 2018). A hackathon is an event usually hosted by tech companies or organizations where participants come together to collaborate on a project often without sleep to achieve their task in a competition style event (Rasmussen University, 2021). The results are the same for both events, to develop a new innovative product. There are techniques that will aid in the productivity and idea generation of a rapid ideation event and by the end of this week I will put these techniques into practice ready for future game jam and hackathon events.

Prototype: Design

For the design of the Stacker prototype, I have created a digital storyboard of the stacker arcade game and how the gameplay mechanics work in general. The rules of the game are the player must stack blocks and reach the top of the grid without losing all of them. Depending on the position of the player the game will set the maximum number of blocks and movement speed. For my game this is called Classic Mode as it retains all the rules of the original arcade game but to put a twist on the classic mode, I have created a game mode called Endless where the player can endlessly stack blocks and increase a counter so they can achieve a high score. I have made this mode as its generally considered bad practice to copy a game in its entirety so to make my game unique I had to put at twist on the existing formula for players to find an interest in the game.

A storyboard of the arcade classic Stacker that I created in Affinity Designer.

Prototype: Development

Before being tasked with this challenge I had already created a main menu scene and have created the game board and in-game menu, which is everything that makes up the game excluding the gameplay mechanics.

Dash and Stack HD remake in development as of 2021 by Fine Chap Games. New main menu (left), new game board (right).

First, I had to make the blocks move and go out of bounds of the screen. I wrote a coroutine that fires a Raycast one block ahead of the first or last child depending on if the player is reversing or not, if the player hits a boundary layer they will reverse. I could have used triggers to track the collisions of the blocks, but I wanted to use a Raycast because firstly I don’t have much experience using Raycasts and it would be beneficial for my development process to get more experience with this element and secondly, I can control a Raycast more precisely than a trigger for example only wanting to gain hits in a specific direction rather than all around the block with a 2D box trigger.

I also moved the boundaries out more so the player blocks can clip outside the grid like the arcade game as my main goal is realism.

Next, I achieved stacking of blocks by having the next spawned block become the active player block and have each block child do a Raycast beneath them to check if there is a block beneath them, if not they will fall to the ground using a coroutine and play a lose animation like the arcade game.

To demonstrate how I utilized a Raycast the gif below will show the debug lines that the ray fires to perform hit checks. Again, I could have used triggers here attached to the block to check when it collides with another block, but I wanted to stay with using Raycasts, this way my code is cleaner and more consistent.

Prototype: Result

I have managed to fully recreate Stacker arcade using a grid, blocks, Raycasts and coroutines. I have replicated the rules and the animations for a classic mode with plans to create an endless mode to create a gameplay twist to differentiate it from the original game. Everything has gone well but if there is one element, I would like to change it would be to rebalance the difficulty, as it stands the speed has 3 settings, a slow speed when you have 3 blocks, a medium speed when you have 2 blocks and a fast speed when you have 1 block. I think that instead of having it this way the speed should gradually increase making the game increasingly difficult the more the player stacks a block. 

Also, due to time constraints I did not manage to match up the lives of the blocks to those on the game menu and this is another element that I would like to change going forward. Overall, the reconstruction went well but for next time I would like to make the game more difficult and make it more featureful.
