Version Control

Before making my next project, I want to reflect on the importance of version control and how it benefits games developers. Version control, also named source control is a method of tracking and managing changes to video game projects, software and more. Software such as GitHub desktop are tools that aid development teams manage changes to their project code over time. Version control keeps track of every modification in an online database and developers have the ability to reverse project version if for example they make a mistake (Atlassian, 2021).

I have used version control for all my games development projects and even to store assets for my company directory but before I knew about it, I used cloud storages such as Dropbox and Google drive and in doing so I often ran into conflicting errors when working in teams and this wasn’t ideal as it would complicate the development process. For every new game development project or project asset directory I will always utilize source control to best manage the pipeline. Upon learning the theme of this week’s rapid ideation, I will create a public repository using GitHub Desktop to manage its development. 

Source control illustration (Chuck Gehman, 2019).

Weekly learning objectives for Week 8: Research (Falmouth University, 2021).

Audience

Knowing your audience when developing video games is important to know what target demographic you are making it for, to support this Vernon Vrolijk from Good Shepherd Entertainment states "The market is the most crowded it's ever been, so the opportunities are there but the reality is that things are tougher than they've ever been. You have to have a clear vision of what you want to do with a game, you can't make that up as you go along. That's a recipe for failure, for both the developer and the publisher.” (James Batchelor, 2017). Games developers must plan ahead and prepare for a challenging development cycle. For my own video games, I will first ask close family and friends to test my game first before considering enlisting in a game testing service such as Antidote, an online service for playtesting pc and mobile games. 

The antitode playtesting website (Antidote, 2021).

User Research

It is vital when conducting research to know what the intended results will be to deliver on the information that was not known before the tests are conducted. The researcher must know what needs to be researched, how the research will be used and what benefit it will have towards the intended medium (Cohen, 2018).

In video games user research is a core part of modern game development, the goal is to help game designers reach design goals by applying scientific and UX design principles, and by understanding the players. The practice of playtesting is the most known method when conducting games user research, a method where a group of players are observed playing a game and notes are detailed on their behaviour (GRUX, 2021). When gathering user research for my own game I will use plugins such as Google Analytics to gather anonymous data, ensuring that players will know it’s being collected, and will use the information to understand how my game is played and if I could use the data to improve features or gameplay systems.

User research illustration (McLaughlin, 2021).

Ethics 

When conducting research on participants for quantitative and qualitative research ethics must be considered. “Ethics is based on well-founded standards of right and wrong that prescribe what humans ought to do, usually in terms of rights, obligations, benefits to society, fairness, or specific virtues.” (Manuel Velasquez et al., 2010). Examples of ethical issues include informed consent, confidentially and anonymity, identification, and non-traceability and so on. Each of these raises many questions and considerations and there are rarely easy ‘black-and-white’ decisions on ethical matters and researchers must take informed decisions depending on the research case (Cohen, 2018).

Ethics integrity illustration (McLaughlin, 2021).

Prototype: Development

Making a platformer game I understand that the environment is important to the aesthetic, so I started focussing on a few environments for the player to traverse across. Using Aseprite I created a few environments with multiple layers so that I could write a parallax effect that would scroll the individual layers slowly to create a realistic immersive effect for the environment. 

Grasslands

The grasslands are a starting area that is bright and full of trees and foliage. The brightness of the area gives the player an indication that they shouldn’t be worried about the difficulty of the game at this point.

The grasslands environment level.

Forest

The forest is the second area the player will come across with a colour scheme that is darker than that previous area indicating that the difficulty should be greater and more challenging.

The forest environment level.

Caves

The caves are the third area the player will experience on their journey and due to the dark colour, it should pose a difficult challenge to them.

The caves environment level.

Caves

The city is the final area that the player will come across and has mixed signals regarding its colour scheme and symbolism. The area is bright which indicates an easy difficulty, but the ruined city tells a different story that although the colour scheme indicates an unchallenging atmosphere the player is not safe due to the destruction all around them.

The sunken city environment level.
